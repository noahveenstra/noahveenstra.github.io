crafting human centered digital experiences

my process

1
ideation
One of the most important steps in crafting a human centered experience is the beginning, of course. This is where I get to know the client, their needs, and their goals. I also get to know the target audience and what they are looking for. This stage is where I start to brainstorm and come up with ideas that will help us reach the goals of the client and the needs of the audience.

Most of the time, client meetings are prioritized in order to deeply understand their goals and needs. This is where I ask a lot of questions and try to leave no stone unturned. This is also where the whackiest ideas about the product are deeply explored.
2
collaboration
Creating human centered designs requires, well, people! It's essential to have robust conversations with key stakeholders not only involved in developing the product but those who use it. This is where user research is conducted and ideas are generated internally and between the client. This could really be an extension of the ideation phase but adding another person to get a different perspective on the issue we are trying to solve for.
3
iteration
This is where we get to the real design work. Viewing design as simply an iterative processes is critical to finding successful visual solutions for all stakeholders. The length of this phase depends on the breadth of the product along with the timeline for delivery. This phase is filled with internal meetings to design multiple different versions of user flows. There have been times I've designed 9 versions of the same user flow to incorporate more advanced ideas.

Creating more versions allows the product team more options to show the client and get their ideas flowing for what the product could be and what they want for the end product in general. The main tool I use for this process if Figma, but I do use my iPad and freeform often for lo-fi mockups quickly.
4
delivery
Here comes the finish line! This step is critical in either handing designs off to build teams or building the site itself. Thankfully I've been part of projects where I've had the opportunities to do both at The CUBE @ MSU and Avodah Transformations. Each process requires different considerations.

Handing off a design requires tokens, a proper design system, and some collaboration with developer teams to explain the experience so it's transferred properly. While handing off a site to the client, the teams I've worked on emphasize communication. Knowing that the site will not look exactly like the frames we created just because of browser and functionality limitations. Making sure that the client understands this is only the first version of the new site and minor adjustments will be made throughout its lifetime.
